# Aram Lee

**PhD Student in Astronomy · University of British Columbia**

I am a PhD student at UBC working on TNO detection pipelines, supervised by
JJ Kavelaars
(NRC Herzberg). My current research applies a CNN-based detection pipeline
(developed during my MSc at UVic) to CFIS-r survey data to search for
high-inclination TNOs lurking at the edges of the solar system.

The basic idea: instead of hand-engineering what a moving object should look
like, we let a neural network figure it out directly from pixels. It turns out
deep learning is surprisingly good at spotting things that are barely there.

## Research Interests

- TNO and KBO detection methods
- Kuiper Belt Object demographics and orbital structure
- Solar system formation and dynamical evolution
- TNO surface composition and colors

## Current Project

Applying a MobileNet-based detection pipeline (yes, MobileNet,
it was cutting-edge once) to CFIS-r wide-field imaging
to discover high-inclination TNOs. The pipeline achieves 50% completeness
at SNR ≈ 3.4 in individual frames, pushing into territory where traditional
methods start to struggle.

## What Keeps Me Going

During my MSc, I found something. While testing the pipeline on real CFHT
data, several previously unknown solar system objects emerged from the noise,
including at least three new TNO candidates, one moving at ~3.3"/hr and
placing it at roughly 40-50 AU. They haven't been re-observed yet, so formal
orbits will have to wait. But that moment of watching an unknown world
quietly drift across a series of frames, a world no one had ever catalogued,
was something I won't forget.

What draws me to this work is the asymmetry of discovery: a single
extreme TNO, pulled into an unusual orbit by a distant planet, a passing
star, or something we haven't thought of yet, can rewrite our picture of how
the solar system formed. Every new detection is a data point. Every data
point has the power to reshape the story.

## A Bit More

I came to astronomy because it sits at the intersection of almost every
science (physics, chemistry, mathematics, computing) while still managing
to be genuinely romantic. Looking at light that traveled billions of years
to reach a detector we built on a mountain top is not something you get tired of.

When I'm not hunting for faint moving dots, I'm probably traveling, cycling,
gaming, or thinking about things that don't have answers. Victoria spoiled me:
mild winters, perpetually overcast skies, kind people, and a pace of life that
makes you feel like you're allowed to think slowly. Vancouver is trying its best.
